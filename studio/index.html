— A practice in Cascais

Begun in Cascais. Still here.

Maja trained at the Interior Design Institute and the Cascais School of Arts & Design, and spent her first decade designing rooms she could not, in good conscience, hand to outside contractors to build. The plans came back wrong. The finishes came back rushed. The problems came back in year two.

So she stopped. She brought every trade in-house — the joiner, the tiler, the electrician, the plumber — and rebuilt the practice around a single rule: the hand that draws the room is the hand that lays it. No subcontractors. No telephone game. No quiet handoffs.

Today the studio works on a small number of homes a year, in Cascais, Estoril, Parede, and the villages between. Maja is on every site, every week. She replies to every first email herself. She speaks seven languages, in case the home she is building is not in your first.

“Subcontracting would be easier and cheaper. It is also not how Maya’s Homes works — and never will. The precision and quality I promise cannot come from someone else’s hand.”

— Maja Milič

— The practice

How the studio actually works.

Detail — Floor plan, hand-marked

One hand, beginning to end.

Every drawing in this studio is signed by the same person who walks the site on demolition day. Nothing is delegated to a junior, a printer, or an outside firm. The thinking and the building are one continuous gesture.

Detail — Iurie laying floor tiles, Rebelva

Trades that stay.

The four craftsmen who build your home have built every Maya’s Homes home for years. They know her standards by feel. They do not need a foreman shouting across a site to get a corner right.

Detail — Final walkthrough, Figueiras

We never really leave.

A bulb in year three. A hinge in year five. The home you live in is a home Maja still answers the phone for. The relationship outlasts the project — that is the design.
