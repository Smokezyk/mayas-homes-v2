— The Place

A studio that only works here.

The studio has built homes in six villages along the same fifteen kilometres of coast for twenty-five years. We know which apartment buildings have damp on the north side, which streets the calçada was last relaid in, and which timber breaks down in two summers of salt. None of that is on a brochure.

— On materials

Salt eats almost everything.

Atlantic air does what no test rig in Northern Europe simulates. Untreated steel rusts in months. Cheap chrome bubbles in a year. Plywood swells if it isn’t sealed on six sides. Even fashionable terrazzo can dust if its binder isn’t right for the climate.

The studio works with a small list of suppliers we’ve broken things for, over a long enough period to know what survives. Bronze, brass, certain hardwoods sourced from local mills, ceramic from two specific kilns, stone from one yard above Sintra. The shortlist is short on purpose.

— On finishes

Warm, not polished.

The Cascais light is low and lateral most of the year. High-gloss surfaces catch it badly — they read clinical. Matte and lightly textured finishes hold the room together, especially in the months when the sun never quite climbs above the rooftops opposite.

Limewash on plaster, oiled timber, honed marble, brushed stone. The vocabulary is consistent across our projects because the climate insists on it.

— On the light

A coast that runs east to west.

The Cascais coastline runs east-west, which means morning sun hits south-facing rooms at a low angle and evening sun reaches living rooms long after dinner. Apartments oriented to the sea read entirely differently in summer and in winter. We design around this — north-facing rooms get the warmer materials, south-facing rooms get the calmer ones. It is rarely the choice a London architect would make on paper, and it is almost always right by April.

— On permits

The town hall knows the studio.

Cascais Câmara permits run on their own timetable. We file as we draw, not as we build, which means most of our permits are approved before the first wall comes down. We have not lost a permit cycle to bureaucracy in over a decade.

— On listed buildings

When a wall cannot move.

Several of the buildings we work on are inside the conservation district. Listed facades stay. Internal layouts are negotiated, room by room, with the heritage office. We have done this enough times to know which arguments work and which do not.
